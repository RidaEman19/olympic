Olympic FAQs

Quick answers about the Games—history, rules, hosting, medals, and more.

The Summer and Winter Olympics are held every four years, alternating every two years. For example, Winter Games occur two years after the previous Summer Games.

The International Olympic Committee (IOC) administers the Olympic Movement, sets eligibility and program rules, and selects host cities, working with local organizing committees and international sports federations.

Cities engage in a multi-year dialogue and evaluation process with the IOC, presenting plans for venues, sustainability, legacy, financing, and community support before a final selection is approved.

Yes. Most sports allow professional athletes, provided they meet qualification and eligibility criteria set by their international federations and the IOC.

Qualification is sport-specific and typically involves meeting performance standards or earning quota spots via world rankings and designated events. National Olympic Committees select the final teams from qualified athletes.

The interlaced rings symbolize the union of five regions of the world and the meeting of athletes in fair competition. The colors were chosen so every nation’s flag contains at least one of them.

The program changes over time. The IOC can add or remove sports and events, sometimes including host-proposed sports for a single edition to reflect local interest and global appeal.

Summer Games feature sports typically played on fields, tracks, courts, and pools. Winter Games focus on ice and snow disciplines like skiing, skating, and sliding sports, using cold-weather venues.

Gold, silver, and bronze medals are awarded to first, second, and third place. Medal tables summarize totals by National Olympic Committee, though official rankings prioritize individual performance over national standings.

The flame is lit in Olympia, Greece, and travels via a relay to the host city, symbolizing continuity between ancient and modern Games and the spirit of peace and unity.
